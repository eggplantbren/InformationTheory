\begin{frame}{Probability Theory}
Information theory is built upon probability theory, like an extra `layer'
that is automatically present whenever you use probabilities.
In the first week, we will spend some time revising probability theory.
\end{frame}


\begin{frame}{Probability Theory: Sum and Product Rules}
The sum rule of probabilities is
\begin{align}
P(A \vee B \given C) &= P(A \given C) + P(B \given C) - P(A, B \given C).
\end{align}

\pause

Here, $A$, $B$, and $C$ are any three statements or propositions.
The vertical bar means `given'. The $\vee$ means `logical OR'.
The comma means `logical AND'.

\pause

Throughout the equation, $C$ is the background context.

\end{frame}



\begin{frame}{Probability Theory: Sum and Product Rules}
The product rule of probabilities is
\begin{align}
P(A, B \given C) &= P(A \given C)P(B \given A, C).
\end{align}

\pause

You might be used to calling $A$, $B$ and $C$ {\em events}, defined as subsets
of the {\em sample space}. The logical OR becomes set union $\cup$,
and the logical AND becomes set intersection $\cap$. You are welcome to use
that terminology and notation, but it is not my preference.

\end{frame}


\begin{frame}{Probability Theory: Sum and Product Rules}
If $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive (given $C$), the sum rule simplifies
to
\begin{align}
P(A \vee B \given C) &= P(A \given C) + P(B \given C).
\end{align}

\pause

If $A$ and $B$ are independent (given $C$), the product rule simplifies to

\begin{align}
P(A, B \given C) &= P(A \given C)P(B \given C).
\end{align}


\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Probability Distributions}
Probability is usually used after defining a hypothesis space
(traditionally called a sample space), which is a set of mutually
exclusive and exhaustive `atomic' propositions. For example,
if $x$ is either 1, 2, or 3, we have three propositions,
and their probabilities are given by a probability mass
function $p(x)$, where $p(i) = P(x = i)$.\pause

This is a `discrete random variable' formally, though we won't
use that terminology much. A random variable just means
that a probability distribution has been defined.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Sum and Product Rules: Example}
Suppose $x$ is either 1, 2, or 3, with probabilities
$(0.2, 0.1, 0.7)$ respectively. Find:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(a)] $P(x \geq 2)$.
\item[(b)] $P(x = 2 \given x \geq 2)$.
\end{enumerate}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Continuous Probability Distributions}
Continuous probability distributions may also be defined
by a probability density function $p(x)$. Probabilities
are obtained via the sum rule which becomes an integral:

\begin{align}
P(x \in A) &= \int_A p(x) \, dx.
\end{align}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Integration $\leftrightarrow$ Summation Duality}
In most cases, if we have an equation that involves a summation symbol
for the discrete case ($\sum$), the equivalent equation for the continuous
case involves an integral ($\int$).
Informally, we can say
\begin{align}
\sum \equiv \int
\end{align}\pause
When writing general things that apply to both, we can choose either the
summation symbol or the integration symbol, and once we have a real context,
we will know which one it is.

\end{frame}


\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Expected Values}
For an unknown quantity $x$ with probability distribution $p(x)$,
the expectation is
\begin{align}
E(x) &= \sum x p(x).
\end{align}
The sum is over all possible values. \pause
More generally, we have
\begin{align}
E(g(x)) &= \sum g(x) p(x).
\end{align}\pause
\pause
In general, in this course, integration or summation without
any specified limits means integration or summation over the
whole domain (known from the context).

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Variance and Standard Deviation}
Variance and standard deviation are defined by

\begin{align}
\textnormal{Var}(x) &= \sum \left(x - E(x)\right)^2 p(x) \\
\textnormal{sd}(x) &= \sqrt{\textnormal{Var}(x)}.
\end{align}

\pause
We will hardly ever use these.

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Entropy}
The fundamental new quantity introduced in information theory
is called the {\bf entropy} or the {\bf Shannon entropy}.
You may have heard of it in physics,
and there is a relationship which we will explore later in the
course. The definition is
\begin{align}
H(x) &= -\sum p(x) \log p(x).
\end{align}
\pause

{\bf Note}: Always remember the minus sign. Later, we will
see some similar quantities that are defined without the minus
sign. Try to keep it straight!
\end{frame}


\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Entropy}
The entropy is written as a function of $x$, but it's not
a function of the actual value of $x$, but rather of the
probability distribution that has been assigned to $x$.\pause

Entropy is commonly said to describe the {\bf amount of uncertainty} in the probability distribution.
\end{frame}


\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Entropy: Special cases}
Two special cases, which are very easy to prove
(you should do it as an exercise),
will help us build up intuition about entropy.
\pause
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] If $x$ is known, i.e., there is a single
possible value that has probability 1, then $H(x) = 0$. \pause
\item[(ii)] If $x$ has a discrete uniform distribution,
i.e., there are $N$ possibilities with probability $1/N$ each,
then the entropy is $\log(N)$.
\end{enumerate}
\pause

{\bf Note}: We get to choose the base of the logarithm.
If we choose base 2, the units of entropy are {\bf bits}.
If we choose the natural logarithm (base $e$), the units
are {\bf nats}.

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Entropy for Continuous Distributions}
Let $x$ be unknown with a probability density function $p(x)$.
The Shannon entropy may still be defined, as
\begin{align}
H(x) &= -\int p(x) \log p(x) \, dx.
\end{align}
\pause
Sometimes people call this the `differential entropy'.
It basically works the same as in the discrete case,
but with some change to the interpretation.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Entropy (Continuous Version): Special cases}
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] If $x$ has a continuous uniform distribution,
i.e., there is a region of possibilities with volume $V$
and the density is $1/V$,
then the entropy is $\log(V)$.
\item[(ii)] If $x$ is known and has a Dirac delta function
$\delta(x - x_{\rm true})$ as its PDF,
the entropy is $-\infty$.
\end{enumerate}
\pause

{\bf Note I}: In the continuous case, entropy can be negative.
It's just because volumes can be less than 1.\\
{\bf Note II}: Under transformations of the parameter space,
e.g., $x' = h(x)$, continuous entropy is not invariant.
As with volumes, Jacobian factors get involved.
\end{frame}
