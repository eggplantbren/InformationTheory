\begin{frame}{Probability Theory: Sum and Product Rules}
The sum rule of probabilities is
\begin{align}
P(A \vee B \given C) &= P(A \given C) + P(B \given C) - P(A, B \given C).
\end{align}

\pause

Here, $A$, $B$, and $C$ are any three statements or propositions.
The vertical bar means `given'. The $\vee$ means `logical OR'.
The comma means `logical AND'.

\pause

Throughout the equation, $C$ is the background context.

\end{frame}



\begin{frame}{Probability Theory: Sum and Product Rules}
The product rule of probabilities is
\begin{align}
P(A, B \given C) &= P(A \given C)P(B \given A, C).
\end{align}

\pause

You might be used to calling $A$, $B$ and $C$ {\em events}, defined as subsets
of the {\em sample space}. The logical OR becomes set union $\cup$,
and the logical AND becomes set intersection $\cap$. You are welcome to use
that terminology and notation, but it is not my preference.

\end{frame}


\begin{frame}{Probability Theory: Sum and Product Rules}
If $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive (given $C$), the sum rule simplifies
to
\begin{align}
P(A \vee B \given C) &= P(A \given C) + P(B \given C).
\end{align}

\pause

If $A$ and $B$ are independent (given $C$), the product rule simplifies to

\begin{align}
P(A, B \given C) &= P(A \given C)P(B \given C).
\end{align}


\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\begin{exampleblock}{Context --- Fruit Example I}
A large shipping container contains some fruit --- apples, bananas, and
cherries. Suppose that 20\% of the pieces of fruit are apples, 10\% are
bananas, and 70\% are cherries.
\end{exampleblock}\pause

\begin{block}{Question}
What proportion of the pieces of fruit are either apples or cherries?
\end{block}

\end{frame}%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

\begin{frame}{Probability}%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\begin{block}{Question}
What proportion of the pieces of fruit are either apples or cherries?
\end{block}\pause

\begin{exampleblock}{Solution (common sense!)}
(proportion that are either apples or cherries) = \\
\quad(proportion that are apples) + 
     (proportion that are cherries)
\end{exampleblock}\pause

\begin{exampleblock}{Solution (mathematics notation!)}
\vspace{-1em}
\begin{align}
P(A \vee C) &= P(A) + P(C) \\
            &= 0.2 + 0.7   \\
            &= 0.9. 
\end{align}
\end{exampleblock}

\end{frame}






\begin{frame}{Probability}%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
Let's now consider a different problem.\pause

\begin{exampleblock}{Context --- Fruit Example II}
I have a single piece of fruit in my bag, and you know it is
either an apple, a banana, or a cherry.
Suppose you are 20\% certain that it is an apple, 10\% certain
that it is a banana, and 70\% certain that it is a cherry.
\end{exampleblock}\pause

\begin{block}{Question}
How certain are you that it is either an apple or a cherry?
\end{block}

\end{frame}%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%







\begin{frame}{Probability}%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\begin{block}{Question}
How certain are you that it is either an apple or a cherry?
\end{block}\pause

\begin{exampleblock}{Solution (common sense!)}
(certainty that it is an apple or cherry) = \\
\quad(certainty that it is an apple) + 
     (certainty that it is a cherry)
\end{exampleblock}\pause

\begin{exampleblock}{Solution (mathematics notation!)}
\vspace{-1em}
\begin{align}
P(A \vee C) &= P(A) + P(C) \\
            &= 0.2 + 0.7   \\
            &= 0.9. 
\end{align}
\end{exampleblock}\pause

Look familiar?
\end{frame}


\begin{frame}{Probability}%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
We just saw that two {\bf very different problems} could be solved by
applying {\bf exactly the same mathematics}. That mathematics was
actually the {\bf sum rule of probability theory}.\pause

The first example was about the relative `sizes' of sets and
subsets.\pause

The second example was about reasoning when there is some
uncertainty involved.

\end{frame}%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%



\begin{frame}{Probability}%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
The first example was about the relative `sizes' of sets and
subsets. \pause 
\begin{exampleblock}{Sizes of sets and subsets}
Classical or `frequentist' methods apply probability theory
on problems like this {\bf only} (though one can stretch the
imagination to make many problems seem like this).
\end{exampleblock}\pause

The second example was about reasoning when there is some
uncertainty involved. When probabilities are used this way,
it is called `Bayesian'. We will mostly adopt this interpretation
throughout the course.

\end{frame}%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
